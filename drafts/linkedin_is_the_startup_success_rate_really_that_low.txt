"The startup success rate is very low."

True. Also: completely irrelevant to your specific situation.

Here's the distinction that matters:

That statistic describes a group average - what happened to a large pool of entrepreneurs in the past. It says nothing about what will happen to you, specifically, as you keep learning and iterating.

Two reasons the number misleads:

1. The pool isn't uniform. Some founders had deep domain experience, technical moats, distribution channels. Some were building from imagination alone, for the first time. Averaging all of them produces a number that hides everything important.

2. Entrepreneurs aren't static. They learn. A static figure can't predict a moving target.

There's also a training gap no one talks about: entrepreneurship is something we were never systematically taught. 12+ years of school trained us to follow rules, pass exams, enter systems. Entrepreneurship has no curriculum, no framework, no walkthrough.

So "the success rate is low" is really describing: a large group of undertrained people sitting an exam with no textbook. That's not evidence of individual failure. It's a systemic gap in preparation.

Most importantly: entrepreneurship isn't a lottery.

Lotteries have independent events - the last draw doesn't affect the next. Entrepreneurship is the opposite. Every action feeds directly into the next decision. Which means failure isn't pure loss - as long as you're still in the learning process, a failed attempt narrows the error space for everything that follows.

The right question isn't "what are my odds?" It's:

- Am I consistently getting useful feedback?
- Am I attributing outcomes accurately?
- Am I genuinely updating my judgment after each action?

For someone who keeps answering yes - the odds shift over time.

The most valuable founders aren't those who fail least. They're the ones whose judgment improves most noticeably after each iteration.
